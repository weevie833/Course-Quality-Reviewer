Overview: Cultivating Relationships

It is not enough to simply write a strong grant narrative. Rather, the narrative is merely one piece in a proverbial puzzle that requires thoughtful and proactive management. To some extent, a grant writer is a project manager.

With this assumption in mind, grant writing may be described as a process that requires a variety of inputs, including consultation with key organizational stakeholders. The objective behind this process is to ensure a well-written grant narrative, proper background documents, and cultivated relationships with prospective and current funders that position the organization for continued success.

Anticipated outcomes from a well-executed grant writing process include a high degree of trust in the organization as well as long-term commitments to its mission, values, and funding priorities. This process should include a robust acknowledgement to the funder and external communications that highlight the impact of the gift on the organization’s mission, programs, services, and outcomes. Such communications could include, but not be restricted to: e-blasts, press releases, blogs, digital and printed appeal campaigns, and social media updates.

Additional Considerations

More than external communication, however, this process should also include management of the grant itself after an award has been given. Although the intricacies of grant management lie outside the purview of this class, it is a crucially important consideration for an organization. Are staff executing the project or plan as intended? Are outcomes on track? Does the funder know how the project/program is doing? If the gift supported organizational capacity, what are the indicators that the gift is working as intended? When a grant has been awarded, the job is not complete. Rather, the work for the organization has just begun.
