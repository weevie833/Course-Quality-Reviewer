Welcome to CMPL 850 – Managing Information Technology Capstone.

In this course, you will complete an encapsulated IT management project, meaning a structured end-to-end initiative that represents the type of strategic technology challenge an IT leader might face in a professional setting—but executed within a defined academic framework.

Think of this course as a real-world simulation, a project in which you're expected to plan, analyze, manage, and reflect on a technology initiative from start to finish. This might involve improving an IT process, managing a digital transformation effort, implementing a security policy, or solving a cross-functional IT-business challenge. What makes this “encapsulated” is that it's self-contained within the 16-week course structure: you’ll define the scope, develop the approach, track progress, and present outcomes within a single academic term.

Unlike a traditional research paper or exam-based course, your capstone will draw upon everything you’ve learned across the program—leadership, risk management, project planning, security, and data strategy—but it will also require you to meet academic standards for analysis, research, and documentation. This means you'll apply graduate-level research methods, cite scholarly and industry sources, and submit reflective writing that demonstrates critical thinking and professional growth.

In short, this capstone is both a leadership-driven technology project and an academic demonstration of your learning and readiness to lead in the IT field. You’ll finish the course not only with a grade but with a portfolio-worthy artifact that reflects your readiness to manage real-world IT challenges.

Using Program Learning Outcomes as a Reflective Lens

As part of your Capstone experience, you’ll not only complete an applied IT management project, but you’ll also reflect deeply on how your learning across the entire M.S. in Leadership: Information Technology Management program has prepared you for this work.

The Program Learning Outcomes (PLOs) represent the key competencies that define the goals of this graduate program. Throughout the course, and especially in your reflective assignments, you will use these outcomes as a lens for analyzing and articulating your development as an IT leader.

Here’s how you’ll engage with the PLOs:

  • At the start of the course, you’ll review the eight PLOs and identify which ones are most closely aligned with your chosen project.
  • As your project progresses, you’ll be asked to connect your planning, decision-making, and execution strategies to specific PLOs, for example, how you’re applying project management principles (PLO 2) or how your solution supports innovation through emerging technologies (PLO 8).
  • At the end of the course, your final reflection will explicitly address how your capstone project demonstrates your mastery of these outcomes. You’ll reflect on what you’ve learned, where you’ve grown, and how your project illustrates your readiness to lead in the IT field.

Using the PLOs in this way helps you demonstrate that your capstone is more than a standalone project—it’s a culmination of everything you’ve accomplished in the program. It also gives you language you can use to articulate your skills and value in professional settings, such as interviews, performance evaluations, or leadership conversations.

Academic Standards for Research, Writing, and Citation

As a graduate-level Capstone course, CMPL 850 – Managing Information Technology Capstone upholds rigorous academic standards in research, writing, and citation. The quality of your work should reflect both professional integrity and scholarly discipline. The following expectations will guide your assignments, weekly reflections, and final project submissions:

Research Standards

  • Use credible, scholarly, and industry-recognized sources to support your project decisions and reflections.
  • Peer-reviewed journal articles, white papers, professional association publications (e.g., IEEE, ACM, Gartner), and academic books are expected.
  • Wikipedia, non-reviewed blogs, or AI-generated content without verification should not be used as primary sources.
  • Demonstrate depth by integrating both theoretical perspectives and current best practices from the IT and leadership fields.

Writing Standards

  • All written work should be clear, concise, logically structured, and professionally presented.
  • Maintain a formal academic tone while articulating practical applications of IT leadership concepts.
  • Use proper grammar, punctuation, and spelling throughout all submissions.
  • Avoid first-person narration unless explicitly allowed for reflection-based components.

Citation Standards

  • All citations and references must follow APA 7th edition formatting.
  • In-text citations should be properly matched with full references at the end of each document.
  • Paraphrased material must be clearly attributed; direct quotes should be used sparingly and cited appropriately.
  • Plagiarism—including failure to properly cite paraphrased ideas—will result in academic review and possible disciplinary action.

Submission Expectations

  • Submit all work using the designated templates and platforms.
  • All major submissions (e.g., project plans, progress reports, final deliverables) must include citations and a reference page.
  • Late or incomplete work must follow the course policy for extensions or alternative arrangements.

These standards ensure that your capstone reflects the professionalism, rigor, and critical thinking expected of leaders in the IT management field.

Academic Standards for CMPL 850 Capstone Project

Project Design

  • Clearly define the project scope, objectives, deliverables, and success criteria.
  • Align the project with one or more Program Learning Outcomes (PLOs).
  • Include a rationale supported by scholarly and industry sources (minimum of 3 peer-reviewed or authoritative references).
  • Justify the project’s relevance to the field of IT leadership and its practical value.
  • Submit a professionally formatted project plan using the provided template.
  • All written content must follow APA 7th edition standards for formatting, in-text citations, and references.

Project Management

  • Demonstrate the use of project management principles (e.g., timeline, milestones, risk analysis, stakeholder engagement).
  • Use the provided weekly project tracking template to document progress, barriers, changes, and decisions.
  • Incorporate ethical considerations, leadership strategies, and alignment with organizational or simulated goals.
  • Reference project management frameworks or models (e.g., PMBOK, Agile, or other applicable methodologies).

Status Reporting

  • Submit weekly updates using the capstone template, including:
  • Completed tasks
  • Upcoming tasks
  • Challenges or changes
  • Reflection on progress and decision-making
  • Status updates should reflect critical thinking, self-assessment, and application of course content.
  • Reports must be written in a professional tone and follow APA v7 formatting for any cited content.

Debrief with Stakeholders

  • Conclude the project with a written or recorded debrief that simulates reporting to key stakeholders (e.g., executives, clients, or IT teams).
  • Clearly communicate outcomes, lessons learned, recommendations, and reflections on leadership and collaboration.
  • Support analysis with references to project results, scholarly sources, and course concepts.
  • Use professional language, organized presentation structure, and visual aids if applicable (charts, dashboards, summaries).
