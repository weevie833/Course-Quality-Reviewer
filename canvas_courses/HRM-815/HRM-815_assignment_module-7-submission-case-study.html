Case Title: Monitoring Missteps or Justified Action?

Jessica is a marketing manager at a mid-sized tech company. Over the past few months, her supervisor noticed a decline in her productivity and responsiveness to client emails. Concerned about her performance, the company’s IT department was asked to review her computer activity. The review revealed that Jessica had been spending significant time during work hours on personal social media and streaming platforms.

The company has an electronic use policy outlined in the employee handbook, which allows for monitoring of employee computer use. However, Jessica claims she was never clearly informed of the extent to which her activity could be tracked. When confronted, she admitted to personal use but argued that her work was still being completed on time and that she often worked extra hours after standard business hours.

Based on this information and a pattern of recent underperformance, Jessica’s employment was terminated. She is now threatening to pursue legal action, claiming her privacy was violated and that the termination was unfair and retaliatory.

Case Questions:

  1. Did the company violate Jessica’s privacy rights by monitoring her computer activity? Why or why not?
  2. Was the termination handled fairly and legally? What steps should have been taken to ensure due process?
  3. What policies or procedures should be in place to avoid similar conflicts in the future?

If you were the HR manager in this situation, part of handling it professionally would involve creating a formal written communication documenting the company’s position and actions taken. This could be in the form of:

  • A termination letter (if communicating directly to the employee)
  • An internal investigation summary (for leadership and legal counsel)
  • A memo to leadership (outlining the situation, actions taken, and risk assessment)

For this exercise: Prepare a one-page formal document you believe would be most appropriate for this situation, keeping in mind that it may later be used in litigation. In your document:

  • Use clear, professional, and objective language—avoid subjective or emotional phrasing.
  • Include only factual information supported by evidence (dates, policy references, documented performance issues).
  • Reference the relevant company policy (e.g., electronic use monitoring) and confirm whether it was communicated to the employee.
  • Clearly note the decision-making process and any steps taken to ensure fairness and compliance with company policy and applicable law.
  • Avoid including speculation, personal opinions, or unrelated performance history that could weaken the company’s position in legal proceedings.
  • Keep the scope of the document limited to what is necessary for the purpose (e.g., avoid unnecessary detail about other employees or unrelated incidents).

When drafting, consider the dual audience:

  1. Internal stakeholders (executives, legal counsel) who need to understand the facts and rationale.
  2. Potential external reviewers (e.g., regulatory agencies, courts) who will scrutinize the document for fairness, legal compliance, and consistency with stated policies.
