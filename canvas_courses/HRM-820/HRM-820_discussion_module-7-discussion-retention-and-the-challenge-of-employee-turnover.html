Recruiting and selecting the right talent is only part of the staffing cycle—retaining that talent is equally critical. High turnover disrupts organizational performance, increases costs, and can damage morale. However, not all turnover is harmful; sometimes it creates opportunities for growth or improves organizational fit. The key is for organizations to distinguish between functional and dysfunctional turnover, understand the drivers behind employee departures, and design evidence-based retention strategies that align with business objectives.

You are the HR manager for a retail company with 200 employees. In the last 12 months, turnover has risen to 35%, mostly among customer service associates. Exit interviews suggest a mix of issues:

  • Pay and scheduling concerns
  • Lack of advancement opportunities
  • A few cases where low performers were hired away by competitors
    Leadership has asked you to recommend strategies to reduce dysfunctional turnover while recognizing that some turnover may actually be functional.

Your Task (300–400 words):

  1. Explain why retention matters. Why is it such a critical piece of organizational staffing strategy?
  2. Analyze the turnover data in this scenario. Which factors point to dysfunctional turnover? Which examples might be functional?
  3. Propose two evidence-based retention strategies that address the core issues in this scenario (e.g., compensation, scheduling flexibility, training, career development, supervisor support).
  4. Share an example from your experience or research where turnover analysis or a retention initiative made a clear impact on organizational outcomes.

As you respond, consider:

  • How retention strategies connect to recruitment and selection methods in supporting organizational objectives and competitive goals.
  • How decision-making frameworks can guide workforce planning for retention and development.
  • The importance of proactive versus reactive approaches in reducing voluntary turnover.
