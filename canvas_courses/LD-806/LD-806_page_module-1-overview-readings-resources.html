Introduction to Fundraising & Development

Fundraising is generally viewed as ‘asking for money’. This is essentially true. Whether you have been asked to support the local Little League baseball team or received a printed mailer that asked for your support to build a homeless shelter, you have likely been the recipient of a fundraising activity. 

Fundraising, however, is not an end in itself. Rather, it is the servant of philanthropy. It draws its meaning and essence from the ends that are served: caring, helping, healing, nurturing, guiding, uplifting, teaching, creating, preventing, advancing a cause, preserving values, etc. 

Fundraising is supported by the process of development. Broadly defined, development is the careful nurturing and building of relationships over time between organizations and donors/philanthropists. In a sense, development could be seen as separate from fundraising. Effective development often results in meaningful relationships that could be characterized as friendships that are essential to the long-term sustainability of organizations themselves. 

Building an Internal Constituency

Effective fundraising (and development) requires a substantial amount of input from different kinds of ‘players’ throughout an organization. For example, a fundraising/development professional needs an accurate understanding of the accounting picture in order to communicate fiscal realities to donors/philanthropists. 

Development should entail ‘’buy-in’ from the organization’s leadership whose vision helps to paint the proverbial picture the fundraising/development professional is ‘selling’ to donors/philanthropists. Effective development also entails that the fundraising/development professional solicit internal support from managers, for instance, who can provide insights into ways in which financial support can ‘transform’ some aspect of what they do, or lives served by their program. 

For example, a fundraiser might ask a manager what an increase in funding for a particular project might mean for an individual served by their program. The manager’s input could serve as the basis for a case study that enables prospective donors to picture their impact.

At its core, effective development relies on internal relationships throughout the organization to produce actionable information that enhances every aspect of the fundraising and donor stewardship process.
