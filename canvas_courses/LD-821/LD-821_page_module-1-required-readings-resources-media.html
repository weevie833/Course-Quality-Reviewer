Overview

One of the most powerful starting points for examining the intersection of critical thinking and ethics comes from the teachings of the renowned Greek philosopher Socrates. He championed the importance of thinking clearly, rationally, and objectively to the best of our ability especially in response to the cultural climate of his time, when Sophism, with its focus on persuasion over truth, was highly influential.

Through Plato’s writings, we learn that Socrates believed in the existence of objective Truth, accessible through the pursuit of knowledge and education. Living in a time when relativism shaped many societal norms across Greek city-states, Socrates challenged this view by arguing that life’s decisions must align with the pursuit of what he called the “Good.” This Good could be discovered through continuous questioning, a method that seeks evidence and understanding beyond personal biases or subjective impressions.

Socrates taught that an objective Truth exists independent of human perception and that reason and self-knowledge help reveal this Truth over time. Critical thinking, in this view, becomes a disciplined practice of examining assumptions, seeking clarity, and aiming toward universal values rather than individual desires.

Plato built upon this by proposing that humans carry innate knowledge of “Ideals”—universal forms such as Goodness, Justice, and Beauty—that guide our moral reasoning. These ideals are not arbitrary or purely subjective; rather, they exist within a hierarchy of values that define what it means to live ethically and meaningfully. While each person’s path to discovering the Good may differ, the pursuit itself must be grounded in reason, virtue, and moderation.

The world offers us clues to these ideals, though it remains imperfect. True Goodness is found not in convenience, but in harmony, virtue, and rational thought. The values we inherit across generations are part of this broader human striving—not simply personal opinions but reflections of a shared moral framework.

As you explore this module, use these philosophical insights to reflect on your own experiences, both successes and mistakes, along your career and leadership path. Understanding the habits of critical thinking and their connection to ethical living can strengthen your personal vision and leadership effectiveness.
