Overview

As we go further into our study in week #3, it is important to begin to understand how the study of ethics, morality and good decision-making go hand-in-hand.  Part of this work is steeped in our ability to identify important values that we should build on.  

One could argue that understanding and creating a moral stance comes from doing what we understand to be the practical content and application of convictions we gain from the world around us and to the people who reside in it.  This approach, best exemplified by the work of Immanuel Kant, is based on the philosophical conception of deontology.  Deontology refers to the belief that morality is only properly understood in the context of moral necessity, or obligation.  Unlike consequentialist theory, deontological thinkers believe that truly right decisions are not based on practical end results, but rather the moral obligation inherent in the conception of duty found in the natural understanding and right employment of the concept of reason.  

How would this change our view of our order if we think in these terms?  In Kant’s theory of the early nineteenth century, he points to two specific components that are the basis of duty-based ethical approaches.  Individuals often refer to the categorical imperative or a universal moral principle that can be upheld regardless of situation and the principle of ends, which argues that the ethical right decision must be based on the belief that the process of carrying out proper moral decision making is more important or just as important as the proper, reasoned end.  Individuals who approach moral problem-solving from this perspective, therefore, base their moral decision making on their obligation to an absolutistic statement found in the base conception of this obligation to principles.  

As you go through this week, think of what your stance is and how you can begin to formulate your own understanding. 

As we start evaluating various industries, we need to be aware of the threads of how our current system in our own society and the globe operate.  This includes starting the process of describing where leaders and organizations struggle in formulating strong ethical positions and follow through.

To study this well, we need to study the ideologies that have come to frame the values at play.  When we look at capitalism and democracy from the standpoint of their true intentions, namely to promote the betterment of those who participate in the systems above, we come to understand the importance of ethical values and the need to return to  those values in order to “right” the “wrongs” that seem to have infiltrated both ideologies.  We can make strides forward.  

In analyzing Smith’s work, Younkins writes in the article Adam Smith’s Moral and Economic System, that true capitalism must be built upon the concept of the “prudent” individual who demonstrates self-control and cultivates habits that emphasize unselfish inclinations.  It is in this reciprocity of exchange and the moral concern that focuses on the greater concern, that “free-market” systems prosper as it is understood that one’s prosperity is directly intertwined with the prosperity of others.  As such, freedom in political, economic and social terms reinforces the important moral concept of “rights and responsibilities” both in the individual and collective ideology.  He writes “The lower virtue of prudence guides the virtuous man in the pursuit of his own well-being.

The commercial man is part of virtuous man and performs his proper function when he attends to his own happiness by pursuing fundamental goods such as property, health, and reputation. In part, he desires wealth for the approval it will attain for him in the eyes of other men who gain pleasure when they see him as successful and productive. A prudent man demonstrates self-command when he denies himself present pleasure for future pleasures that he believes will be greater. A prudent man's habits of economy, industry, attention, discretion, frugality, and application of thought are self-interested and praiseworthy. A prudent man realizes that production is good, that labor is more productive when it is more specialized, and that the more people involved in mutual exchange, the more specialized each person can be” (Younkins, 2005).

In conclusion, experts relay that if we are to curb many of the potentially destructive and unethical trends and habits that have developed in the world to this extent, we need to return to the core of what these two important system concepts were built upon--the notion that the responsibility of proper moral conduct is important and that ethical leadership is the key in helping us redefine how we ought to live, making a difference in our collective future.  Various components of the market can help in that endeavor and leadership is essential to its success.
