Overview

This module we will turn our attention to the very crux of ethical theory by exploring the ideologies of objective and subjective thought.

Ethical evaluation helps to enrich our discourse of any issue by forcing us to more carefully assess our own inner interpretations (perhaps subjective) as those concepts intersect the external world (perhaps objective) that is around us. To do this well, we need to be more in tune with a few philosophical components.

First, it is important to be sure to analyze in greater depth the idea of concepts and language. Every aspect of our communication is linked to our thinking. Becoming more careful about what concepts influence us and how we often default in our thinking to certain thinking patterns or concepts is discovered in a thorough exploration of ethical evaluation. To begin this process, we need to be able to check our own reasoning or thought process. Looking at the logic of an argument or conclusion is very important.

Second, ethics can also help us be more aware of the different characteristics of reasoning humans can employ. Perhaps the most important to consider is the difference between deductive and inductive thinking. Deductive reasoning centers upon the idea that there is a guarantee of truth concluded if the premises or initial observations are true and line up in logical form with the end conclusion.

Inductive conception is formulated around the idea that premises presented are possibly true and therefore a probable conclusion is true. Thus, the initial observations lend themselves to the strong possibility of truth rather than the guarantee as they connect with the conclusion.

As humans, we know that we use both on a daily basis to interpret information and to formulate our understanding of the world around us. The reasoning components listed above also lead us to the last thinking component enhanced by our study of ethics. Exploring the way we think allows us to look for and make stronger conclusions based upon the practice of analogies.

At the core of good ethical thinking is the ability to make solid comparisons to illustrate a point or sometimes prove a conclusion. In addition, we also look to infer to the best possible explanation; where humans use logic to formulate a solid criteria for a presumed connection between premises and a conclusion. To do this, reason dictates reliance upon the best explanation as a tool to move knowledge forward.

Objectives:

  1. Define the philosophical terminology of objective and subjective thought
  2. Define how the different classifications of thinking help us to better understand others
  3. Contemplate how prominent ethical theory has defined people's viewpoints
  4. Begin to understand how the positive and negative attributes of various positions help us to better evaluate the decisions we make.
