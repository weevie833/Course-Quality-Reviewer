Overview

We continue this week to explore what societies and organizations have used to determine ethical decisions.  As we identify these theories, we also have to take the time to evaluate both the positive and negative features of these various approaches. 

To do this, we will explore seven notions on ethical theory that have come to predominate leadership and institutional decision-making.  All seven argue justified expectations about the benefits other people or society ought to provide; provisions that are, by definition, moral inherently.  Morality, therefore, is determined by the preservation of agreed upon respect for others through mutual cooperation.  Right and wrong are conceptualized within the framework of expectations concerning benefits for individuals as well as people in groups.  For the benefit of all, individuals base their moral conceptions on the practical application of daily life, the vision they have for how it should be carried out. 

One perspective of the seven has become synonymous with our cultural ideology.  The rights perspective emphasizes the preservation and betterment of that life through agreed upon standards or the preservation of rights best understood in the conception of the golden rule, for example.  This ideology can also be understood in the context of Thomas Hobbes’ selfish ethical theory of the seventeenth century, He writes that the negative component of the golden rule is perhaps more productive.  He argues that “not doing for others what you don’t want done to you” is perhaps a more moral way to formulate or work towards a more moral society. 

Not getting in each other’s way, by not attempting to treat others as you would like to be treated, though a demonstration of rights based moral theory, more accurately respects individual desires and rights as individuals, so that people do not infringe on others’ interest, nor violate their rights and/or their ability to adhere to proper moral principles.   This approach to morality relies upon a base agreed upon understanding of what is important to most involved in the moral decision making process, and can often be seen in the formulation of laws that confirm the preservation of this conception of right acquisition. 

There are six others for you to explore as well.  Though they are all distinctly different there is no doubt that they overlap and you should look for areas where they come together or can be used in collaboration. 

As we enter the last few weeks of the course, it is important to think about how our leadership will intersect our world system.  It is equally beneficial to consider how we, regardless of our industry and situation, can make a difference with ethical leadership by being more aware of the factors that lead to unethical behavior and outcomes. 

It seems that our world has come to an important crossroad.  The issues of expanding globalization, disproportionate economic prosperity, problematic social issues and issues with market monopolization, unfair trade and resource acquisition has caused many people to question what our future as a world community will hold.  As we have made some instrumental progress in terms of technology and world relations over the course of the last few decades, we have also begun to see increasingly troubling indicators that are sending all of us the message that unless we begin to acknowledge, educate, analyze and then problem solve with legitimate direction in the coming years, we could be facing an escalation of more and more global issues that could cause catastrophic results for all involved. 

As state above, at the root of this conversation is the issue of ethical leadership—an important factor sometimes ignored in the haste of constructing progressive economic theories and enhancing international relationships.  In my studies in the last twenty years or so, I have become more and more convinced that many of the problems of the world that have emerged are increasingly and undoubtedly more complex than anyone could have prognosticated decades ago.  It will take the best and the most creative ideas of the leaders of all fields and disciplines to address these issues constructively. 

In order to do this well, though, I am also convinced more than ever that the work of these leaders needs to be steeped in a re-evaluation of the concepts of capitalism, competition and democracy.  In order to do this well, we need to re-evaluate the basic components of these constructs with greater intensity and pay attention to the ethical elements that seem to be underlying all of these issues.  In doing so, we can be better prepared to make the better more informed decisions in our own capacity as we contemplate our current world’s affairs. 

Objectives:

  1. Consider how institutions in our society have made ethical decisions
  2. Evaluate the positive and negative outcomes of each decision-making model
  3. Analyze the value of the seven major ethical theories in the context of better leadership 
  4. Examine leadership decisions in the historical context of global development 
  5. Assess world values, economic development and systematic conditions that have developed in our world and that trend's correlation with ethical leadership
