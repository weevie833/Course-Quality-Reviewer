Overview

You have made it!  Congratulations on finishing this challenging course!   

As we end our class, you will take a look at the Internal Review Board component of UNH in order to see how ethical leadership, research practices and your eventual capstone project overlap. 

One essential question to consider is:  how do we link sound judgment and strong ethical evaluation with the goal of treating everyone with as much "dignity" as possible?  Taking this question to heart this week, perhaps we should focus on the practical features of good decision-making.  Sound judgment culminates in these characteristics:  the acknowledgment of assumptions, the dissection all moral points, the balancing of emotion and reason, the weighing constructively of others’ interests and the willingness on the part of the leader, to act on his or her decisions. 

All five are the basis of sound ethical judgments.  There are a couple of deductions that can be made in reference to these five solid suggestions that I think make a difference.  All of them demand that the leader scrutinize as many options as possible and balance logic with emotion.  Clearly, there are times to consider the important contribution that emotion may have in a particular decision making process but allowing emotion, like anger, to dominate or control a decision making situation is dangerous.  Emotion can blind one from important factual knowledge and detract from the task at hand. 

Second, there is also the issue of prejudice or bias.  Leaders need to be conscious of the individuals involved in their decision-making.  Leaders must attempt to decrease their own bias and prejudices and focus on the well-being of all involved.  Though it is impossible to do this with complete assurance, it is a goal that is essential to work towards as it guides leaders to avoid arbitrary and stereotypical assessments. 

The last suggestion focuses on good decision making and the concept of action.  Too often, leaders come up with great plans or ideas, but fail in their ability to carry out the plans they have conceived of.  Vision must produce action.  This can be self-defeating and demonstrate poor judgment or an inability to accomplish goals. 

This is particularly true of moral situations.  Too many people take the easy way out by making decisions that they know may not be moral or constructive for those involved, as they are swayed by the decision that they think will require less action and effort on their part.  Concisely, they opt for the decision of less resistance. 

As a thinker, it is imperative that one realize that every decision made has moral implications.  Actions taken, and actions not taken, demonstrate what values or virtues one believes are important.  It is an often overlooked important aspect of building solid thinking skills and is at the core of our work this week.

Objectives:

  1. Consider the value of principles and direction of the IRB
  2. Examine credibility, moral sainthood and morality without hubris as possible helpful clues to working with others
  3. Use productive ethical insights in practice to finish formulating a productive ethical leadership vision
